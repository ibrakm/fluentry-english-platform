## Email 2 — Sent Day 2

**Subject:** The sound that doesn't exist in Arabic (and how to make it)

**Preview text:** This one mistake is costing you credibility in every English conversation.

---

[First Name],

Yesterday I asked you to work on the Have/Be confusion.

Today I want to talk about something that affects your credibility every single time you open your mouth in English.

**The 'P' sound.**

Arabic doesn't have it. Your mouth has literally never needed to make it. So when you say "pen," your brain reaches for the nearest sound it knows — and produces "ben."

Most of the time, people understand you from context. But in these situations, it causes real problems:

- Job interviews: "I have experience in **project** management" → sounds like "broject"
- Professional calls: "Can you **print** this?" → sounds like "brint"
- Introductions: "I'm from **Paris**" → sounds like "Baris"

Here's the fix — and it takes less than 5 minutes:

**The Candle Test:**  
Hold your hand in front of your mouth. Say "pen." You should feel a small puff of air on your hand. That puff IS the 'P' sound. Now say "ben." No puff. That's the difference.

**Practice these sentences today:**
- "Please put the paper in the printer." (5 P sounds)
- "The professor prepared a presentation in Paris."
- "I appreciate your patience and professionalism."

Record yourself on your phone. Listen back. Is there a puff of air on each P? If not, try again.

This is the kind of specific, targeted work that makes the difference between "good enough" English and English that commands respect.

## Email 3 — Sent Day 4

**Preview text:** She used a word she learned in French. It meant something completely different in English.

---

[First Name],

A student of mine — I'll call her Salma — was in a client meeting in Casablanca. The client was British.

She wanted to say that she was "currently" working on the report. But she said "actuellement."

The British client looked confused. In English, "actually" means "in fact" or "to be honest" — not "currently." It completely changed the meaning of her sentence.

This is the False Friend trap. And because you speak French, you are especially vulnerable to it.

Here are the five false friends that cause the most problems for Moroccan professionals:

| French Word | You Think It Means | It Actually Means | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|---|
| Actuellement | Actually | Currently | Currently / At the moment |
| Sensible | Sensible (reasonable) | Sensitive (emotional) | Reasonable |
| Éventuellement | Eventually | Possibly | Eventually = in the end |
| Passer un examen | Pass an exam | Take/sit an exam | Take an exam |
| Rester | To rest | To stay/remain | To rest = se reposer |

**Today's exercise:**  
Write one sentence using each correct English word. Then say them out loud.

Example: "I am currently working on the project report."

## Email 4 — Sent Day 6

**Subject:** Why you freeze when you speak English (it's not what you think)

**Preview text:** More grammar study will not fix this. Here's what actually works.

---

[First Name],

You've been working through the guide for a few days now.

Today I want to address the one thing that almost every Moroccan student tells me in our first session:

*"I understand English perfectly. I can write it. But the moment I try to speak, my mind goes blank."*

This is not a language problem. It's a **processing speed problem.**

When you speak, your brain is doing five things at once:
1. Retrieving vocabulary
2. Applying grammar rules
3. Producing sounds
4. Monitoring what you're saying
5. Processing the listener's reaction

If any one of these is slow, the whole system freezes. And studying more grammar makes it worse — because it adds more rules to check, which slows you down further.

The solution is to **reduce the cognitive load** through specific drills:

**The 60-Second Fluency Drill:**  
Set a timer for 60 seconds. Speak about anything — your day, a film you watched, what you had for breakfast. The rule: do not stop. Do not correct yourself. Do not pause to think of the perfect word. Keep speaking for the full 60 seconds.

Do this every day for one week. By Day 7, you will notice that the pauses are shorter and the words come faster.

**The Chunk Method:**  
Instead of building sentences word by word, learn fixed phrases that you can retrieve instantly:
- "As far as I know..."
- "What I mean is..."
- "That's a good point, but..."
- "I'm not sure about that."
- "Can you explain that to me?"

When you have 50 of these chunks memorised, your speaking speed doubles — because you're retrieving phrases, not constructing sentences.

This is what fluency actually means. Not perfect grammar. Fast retrieval.
